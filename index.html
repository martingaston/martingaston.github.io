Why Now?

It's funny - I really hated school but I love learning, and everything I've been learning recently has pointed me to the same realisation: now's the time to learn code.

I've been enormously lucky to have had the opportunity to spend the last eight years working in two incredibly creative industries, journalism and marketing. Yet more and more I see that the challenges and opportunities in those industries - and I'm willing to wager it's much broader than just those two - is in implementing (and understanding) technological change and new ways to work.

One of the things I most strongly believe in right now is that we shouldn't separate code from our other skillsets. I think we should all learn code, but not to become coders. We shouldn't silo our coders into the corner of a workplace and just try and push certain responsibilites to them, which sadly I've seen happen even in companies that pride themselves on being digital. Coding (which admittedly still feels like wrestling very rigid but incredibly fast machines to me right now - but I'm doing my best) is an unbelivably powerful creative tool we can use to enhance the things we're already great at, interested in and passionate about, all while learning a new way of thinking that will steer us to new interests down the line.

Recent Discoveries

  • I really love this episode of Planet Money which talks about the invention of the electronic spreadsheet. We hear automation discussed a lot right now, and parts of that can be pretty terrifying, but I think there's a lot of opportunity for code to aid us with some of our more repetitive everyday tasks. I had this episode in the back of my mind a lot when I was reading Al Sweigart's brilliant Automate the Boring Stuff in Python.
  • Marijn Haverbeke's Eloquent Javascript is equal parts fascinating, useful and daunting. It's the kind of book where you finish a page and realise you've not understood any of it, only to slowly become cognizant as its words roll around in your head - kind of like how Yoda trains Luke Skywalker in The Empire Strikes Back.
  • HBR's Good Charts made me think a lot about the presentation of information, both directly in terms of storytelling and data visualisation (which is what the book is about) and also in a wider scope, such as how to present technical information (like code) to other departments, clients and stakeholders.
